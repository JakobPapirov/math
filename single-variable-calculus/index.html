Limits & continuity

Limits

A specific value "in" function can be approached from two sides, the negative side (from the left) and from the positive side (from the right). If the limits from the left side and the right side are not equal, then the function has no limit and therefore the function is not continuous and therefore is not differentiable.

The sum of the limits is the limit of the sum. The sum property L+M

The difference of the limits is the limit of the difference. The difference propertyL-M

The product of the limits is the limit of the products. The product property L*M

The quotient of the limits is the limit of the quotient. The quotient property L/M

A constant times a limit is the constant multiple of the limit. The multiple property k*L

The limit of an function raised to the power of r/s is the limit raised to the power r/s, where s is non zero.The exponent property L^(r/s)

Continuity

For a function, f(x), to be continuous a limit, L, has to exist and that limit has to equal f(c) when x = c. A function can be continuous within an interval or at the endpoints/boundarypoints. For the first case the limits has to hold from both sides, but at the endpoints only the limit coming from within the function domain (usually called the intervall (a, b)).
